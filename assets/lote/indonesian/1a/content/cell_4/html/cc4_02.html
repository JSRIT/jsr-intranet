Kenalin is the colloquial (slang) version of kenalkan, which means 'introduction' or 'let me introduce someone'.

In colloquial language, it is common to hear (or read in very informal texts like blogs or teen magazines) the -in suffix used in place of the more formal suffix -kan. (You have already seen the word dinamakan.) Basically, -in is easier and quicker to say.

In formal situations or formal written texts, however, you would always use the -kan form.

Sudah means 'already'. Although it is not officially a question word, sometimes the question word is dropped because the tone of voice makes it clear that it is a question.

'Kamu sudah nonton film ini?'

When asked a question with sudah, it is normal to respond with one of the following:

  • sudah
  • belum

You may hear the two used together. For example:

Sudah punya pacar, belum?

Ini means 'this' or 'this is':

  • Ini teman saya Suci.
  • Ini Diego.
  • Orang ini pintar.

Itu means 'that' or 'that is' and is used in exactly the same way as ini.

Question words are a bit more specific in the types of questions they ask in Indonesian than in English. Knowing what a question word is asking for in Indonesian helps you know what sort of answer to give; for example, berapa will always refer to a number. Here are four question words you will hear in the video. You should try to memorise these by making flashcards or putting a chart up somewhere.

  • apa is a general question word
  • siapa is asking for a person
  • di mana is asking for a place
  • bagaimana is asking for a description.

There is a full list of question words in the kosa kata.

In spoken language, you use a number of phrases that don't really mean anything but which are part of social communication. For example, finishing a statement with 'you know?' in English is usually unnecessary, but it is asking or prompting the other person for affirmation. You will hear the following used in the video:

  • anu
  • lho expresses amazement
  • masa expresses surprise
  • wah
  • ya kan?
  • kok can mean 'why' (slang), or it can just add emphasis.

Here are some other expressions you will hear in the video:

  • maaf
  • tidak apa-apa
  • jadi …

Shaking hands

Did you notice how Brack and Suci shook hands? Unlike in Western culture, where a firm handshake is normal, Indonesians shake hands very softly, almost to the point of it being a hand 'stroke' rather than a shake. A soft handshake shows that you are refined and genteel, whereas a hard handshake indicates that you are not.
